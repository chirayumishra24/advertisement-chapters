Chapter 1.2: From Dhindhora to Digital (The Timeline)

Ads in India evolved from oral announcements like Dhindhora Pitna to dynamic digital formats, mirroring societal and technological shifts. This timeline highlights key milestones, blending tradition with innovation.

 

Traditional Roots: Town Criers and "Dhindhora Pitna"

  • The Practice of Dhindhora: Historically, India used a system of informers who announced important news and messages by beating drums, a practice known as "Dhindhora Pitna".
  • Role of Town Criers:
    These individuals acted as early advertisers. They would draw crowds in villages to announce government messages or inform people about the arrival of merchants and their specific goods.
  • Modern Persistence: Similar practices continue today, such as door-to-door vendors using bells or shouting phrases to announce their arrival. People also still use loudspeakers on bikes, auto-rickshaws, or jeeps to promote products or events.
 

The Print Revolution: Hicky’s Bengal Gazette (1780)

  • India's First Newspaper: The first newspaper advertisements in India appeared in 1780 in the Bengal Gazette.
  • Hicky's Contribution: This was the first English-language newspaper in India, founded and edited by James Augustus Hicky. It was popularly known as Hicky’s Bengal Gazette.
  • Early Iconic Ads: A famous example is for the well-known local hair oil, ‘Jabakusum’. India's first newspaper featured text-based promotions targeting European and local audiences in Calcutta.
 

Evolution of Media: From Murals to Social Media

The medium of advertising has transformed significantly over time to reach larger audiences more effectively:

  • Hand-painted murals and signs: From the early 20th century, vibrant hoardings depicted products like guns or Bollywood icons for shops, booming post-WWI with swadeshi push.
  • Performance Art: Street performances, such as Nukkad Nataks (street plays) and folk songs, were used to spread awareness about services and social causes.
  • Mass Media: Advertising expanded into print (newspapers, flyers, brochures), radio, and television.
  • Radio (1920s-1960s): All India Radio popularized jingles (e.g., Amul, Bianca), reaching rural low-literacy areas effectively.
  • TV (1940s): Doordarshan revolutionized visuals post-1959 launch, with color TV in the 1980s exploding.
  • The Digital Era: Today, social media platforms (Facebook, Instagram, X) have become the most popular tools for advertising both small businesses and major brands.
  • Word-of-Mouth: Despite technological growth, word-of-mouth advertising remains vital for small local businesses, though it is usually limited to a specific locality.

Fun Fact

The first-ever written ad was created in 3000 BC by a slaveholder to find runaway slaves and promote their weapon shop. It was a Papyrus found in the ruins of Thebes, Egypt.

 

Timeline: History of Advertising in India (Recall to Relevance)

1780

  • Jan 29, 1780: First advertisement published in India in The Bengal Gazette (Hicky’s Gazette)

1800s

  • Advertising mainly meant sharing important information (arrivals of ships, sales, deaths, notices, etc.)

1905

  • First billboard in India set up in Mumbai by The Times of India

1907

  • Swadeshi Movement influence (1907–1911) → rise of Indian industries & brand promotion
  • First rotary linotype machine installed by Statesman of Calcutta → improved printing speed

1920s

  • Modern advertising industry begins forming in India

1929

  • First major global advertising agency in India: J. Walter Thompson (JWT) in Mumbai
  • Worked with brands like Ponds, Lever Brothers
  • First Indian ad agency: Indian Advertising Agency launched

1930s–1940s

  • Cinema advertising starts (ads played before movies)

1927

  • Radio broadcasting begins in India

1967

  • Commercial radio advertising introduced in India (All India Radio – experimental basis in Bombay, Nagpur, Pune)

1972

  • Transit advertising begins in India – Mumbai BEST buses

1976

  • Television advertising begins in India
  • Jan 1, 1976: First TV ad in India – Gwalior Suiting
  • Launch of Doordarshan → TV becomes major advertising platform

Late 1980s

  • Street furniture advertising (bus stops, benches etc.)

Early 1990s

  • In-store advertising begins (retail displays, signage in malls)

1980s–1990s (Golden Age of Indian Advertising)

  • Liberalization + global brands entering India → strong creative boom
  • Iconic campaigns: Amul Girl, Liril Girl, Onida Devil ("Neighbour’s envy, owner’s pride")

2005

  • Digital OOH advertising starts in India (digital billboards)

2006

  • First ambient advertising campaign in India – Hindustan Unilever Axe campaign

2010s onwards

  • Digital advertising rapidly grows due to smartphones, higher internet penetration, social media

2022

  • Indian advertising market reaches around ₹743 billion
  • Digital advertising becomes the largest share

Present & Future

  • Advertising evolves with AI-powered marketing, AR campaigns, programmatic advertising, generative AI for personalized ads
  • Increased focus on ethics & privacy
 

Key Learnings: The Evolution of the Messenger

1. The Power of Sound (The Dhindhora Era)

Long before screens, advertising was auditory.

  • Town Criers: Used "Dhindhora Pitna" (beating drums) to gather crowds for government news or merchant arrivals.
  • Persistence: This lives on today through the bells of kulfi vendors or the loudspeakers on rickshaws.

2. The Birth of Indian Print (1780)

The formal advertising industry began with ink and paper.

  • Hicky’s Bengal Gazette: India’s first newspaper (1780) introduced the first printed ads.
  • Targeting: Initially aimed at local and European residents in Calcutta, featuring early brands like 'Jabakusum' hair oil.

3. The Shift to Mass Media (20th Century)

Technology allowed ads to scale from a village square to the entire nation.

  • Outdoor: The first billboard appeared in Mumbai in 1905.
  • Radio (1920s): Bridged the literacy gap by using jingles (audio signatures) that even non-readers could recognize.
  • TV (1976): Visual storytelling exploded with the first TV ad (Gwalior Suiting), leading to the "Golden Age" of creative icons like the Amul Girl and the Onida Devil.

4. The Digital & AI Revolution

Advertising has moved from broadcasting to personalization.

  • Growth: Digital is now the largest share of the market.
  • Tech: Use of AI, Augmented Reality (AR), and social media allows for real-time interaction between brands and consumers.
