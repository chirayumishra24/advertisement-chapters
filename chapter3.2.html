CHAPTER 3.2

DEEP DIVE RESEARCH – THE USP
CORE PRINCIPLE

Once you have found your client, the next step is to uncover the “secret sauce” of their business.

This process turns a generic advertisement into a powerful, targeted message.

1. INTERVIEW SKILLS: FINDING THE USP

WHY IT MATTERS

A Unique Selling Point (USP) is the ONE thing that makes a business stand out from competitors.
If every baker sells bread — why should people choose THIS baker?

THE ART OF THE INTERVIEW
  • Don’t ask: “Is your furniture good?” (They will always say yes)
  • Do ask: “What is the one thing customers always compliment you on?” or “If a customer went to your competitor instead of you, what would they miss out on?”
CATEGORIES OF USP – DISSECTION VIEW
USP Category Example (Bamboo Craftsman) The “Message”
Price “The most affordable hand-woven baskets in the district.” Value for money.
Materials “We only use organic, 3-year-old seasoned bamboo.” Quality and Sustainability.
Durability “Furniture built to last three generations.” Reliability and Trust.
Experience “Hand-crafted using a 100-year-old family technique.” Heritage and Artistry.
PRODUCT/SERVICE RELATED QUESTIONS – PROBE DEEPLY

Ask these questions to find out what makes this business special and to create a strong USP for your advertisement.

  1. What is the nature of the products/services you are offering?
  2. Since how long have you been doing this work?
  3. What is special about your small business or its product/service? What makes it unique?
    • (a) Is it the price that makes it unique?
    • (b) Is it the materials you use?
    • (c) Does the product last longer than others?
    • (d) How does it compare to competitors?
    • (e) Do you provide delivery?
    • (f) Does the shop environment (cleanliness, etc.) make it different?
    • (g) Do you offer after-sales service?
    • (h) Any other?
  4. Which is the most selling product/service?
  5. What is the price range of the product/service?

2. TARGET AUDIENCE PROFILE: KNOWING THE BUYER

CORE PRINCIPLE

You cannot talk to everyone at once. If you try to appeal to everyone, you appeal to no one.
You must create a “Customer Persona”.

THREE PILLARS OF THE AUDIENCE
A. Demographics (The “Who”)
  • Age: Gen Z (style) or parents (safety)?
  • Location: Immediate neighborhood or tourists?
  • Income: Luxury treat or daily necessity?
B. Needs & Pain Points (The “Why”)
  • What problem are they trying to solve?
  • Example: A tailor-made suit isn’t just clothes — it’s confidence for an interview or wedding.
C. Behavior (The “Where”)
  • Where do they spend time? (Instagram, local tea shop, newspaper?)
  • This tells you exactly where to place the ad.

Customer Profile Questions

Who usually buys this product/service? Specify:

  • (a) Age
  • (b) Location (distance from shop/business)
  • (c) What are their areas of work?
  • (d) Have they ever given feedback? If yes, what improvements were made?

Think & Answer

Since advertisements are a powerful way of persuading people to do something, what are the responsibilities of the persons making advertisements, and what are the rights of the audience?

AD-MAN SAYS:

Before contracts existed, many early designers got ZERO payment after doing full work! A contract is your INVISIBLE SHIELD — turn “student favor” into REAL pro protection!

KEY LEARNINGS

The USP is your “Anchor”

Every design choice should point back to the USP. If the USP is “Durability”, don’t use delicate fonts.

Listen More, Talk Less

Let the client speak. A small customer story often becomes the best headline.

Specific > General

“We sell the best bamboo” is weak. “Bamboo chairs that never creak” is a winner.
